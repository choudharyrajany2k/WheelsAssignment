# MSA Review Report — Wheels Inc.

**Overall risk score:** 94


## Definitions Scope
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Legal entity names match incorporation documents, not trade names** (ref: Preamble / MSA opening paragraph / Article 15 / Signature block)
  Wheels is identified only as 'Wheels Inc., a corporation duly organized and existing under applicable law' with no state/jurisdiction of incorporation, no registered address (bracketed placeholder), and no confirmation this is the exact registered corporate name versus a brand/trade name. In fleet leasing, the contracting Wheels entity matters materially because title to Vehicles, the fuel-card issuer relationship, the toll-transponder account holder, and the insurer-of-record must all sit with the same legal entity that owns the fleet. 'Wheels Inc.' as a marketing banner does not guarantee it is the titled owner. The Client side is entirely a placeholder ('[Client Legal Name]').
  → *Recommendation:* Before execution, insert the full registered legal name and state of incorporation for the Wheels contracting entity (e.g., the specific titled-owner subsidiary) and confirm it is identical to the entity named as 'lessor and legal owner' on the Vehicle titles and the Master Lease Agreement. Verify against the certificate of incorporation/good-standing certificate, not the 'Wheels Inc. Fleet Management Solutions' letterhead. Populate the Client legal name from its formation documents and confirm the signatory has authority.
- ⚠️ **[HIGH] Wheels is consistently the Lessor/Service Provider throughout — no role reversal** (ref: Preamble defined-parties block; Article 1.1 ("Master Lease Agreement", "Vehicle", "Fleet"); Article 2.1)
  Substantively Wheels is consistently the Service Provider/Lessor and the Client the Lessee across all operative Articles (2.1, 4-12), and Article 2.1 correctly states Wheels 'shall at all times remain the lessor and legal owner.' However, the party-labeling convention creates real ambiguity risk: Wheels is given THREE labels ('Wheels', 'Lessor', 'Service Provider') and the Client TWO ('Client', 'Lessee'). This is a service agreement, yet Wheels is styled 'Lessor' here even though leasing is governed by the companion Master Lease Agreement. Overloading 'Lessor' into the service contract invites cross-document confusion and a drafting error could silently reverse a role (e.g., a future amendment referring to 'the Lessor' obligations under a service clause). No instance of actual reversal was found, but the triple-label scheme is a latent hazard.
  → *Recommendation:* For the Service Agreement, use a single primary label for each party ('Wheels'/'Service Provider' and 'Client') and drop 'Lessor'/'Lessee' from this document, reserving those defined terms for the Master Lease Agreement where title/lease obligations live. If the Lessor/Lessee labels must be retained for cross-referencing, add an interpretation note that they refer to the parties' roles under the Master Lease Agreement only. Then run a defined-term consistency pass to confirm no service obligation is ever pinned on 'the Lessee.'
- ❌ **[HIGH] Defined terms are used consistently and none are orphaned/undefined** (ref: Article 1.1 vs. Articles 5, 6, 3.3, 5.5, 11.2, 16.1; Schedules A/B)
  Multiple orphaned/undefined and mis-cross-referenced terms: (1) 'Rentals' is used in the definition of 'Invoice', Article 6.1(a), and Article 11.2 but is never defined in Article 1.1 — it is presumably defined in the Master Lease Agreement and picked up by the 1.1 fall-through clause, but this should be confirmed. (2) 'Lease Term' is used repeatedly (Articles 5.2, 5.5, Schedule A) with an initial capital but is not defined in this Agreement — again reliant on the MLA fall-through. (3) 'Physical Damage Waiver' is capitalized and treated as a defined term (5.5, Schedule A) but has no Article 1.1 definition. (4) 'Term' is defined in Article 3.2, and 'Initial Term'/'Renewal Term' there, which is fine, but Article 1 survival (3.3) references 'Article 5.6' and Article 5.5 references 'the notice provisions of Article 8.3' — neither Article 5.6 nor Article 8.3 exists (rate-adjustment notices are in Article 7; Client obligations in 8.1/8.2 only). These are broken cross-references, not just style issues. (5) The 'Service'/'Services' definition points to services 'described in Article 4', but the Services are actually described in Article 5 (Article 4 is Onboarding); Article 2.1/2.3 correctly point to Article 5 in places, creating an internal contradiction. (6) 'Additional Services' appears as an unnumbered heading under Article 5 and is not tied to the defined term structure.
  → *Recommendation:* Fix before execution: (a) Add definitions (or explicit MLA cross-references) for 'Rental/Rentals', 'Lease Term', and 'Physical Damage Waiver' — do not rely silently on the MLA fall-through for terms this central to billing. (b) Correct the 'Service'/'Services' definition to reference Article 5, not Article 4. (c) Repair the broken cross-references: 'Article 5.6' in the survival clause (3.3) should point to the correct billing/reconciliation provision, and 'Article 8.3' in 5.5 should read 'Article 7.2/7.3'. (d) Reconcile every 'Article 4' vs 'Article 5' reference to Services throughout. Run a full defined-term and cross-reference audit given the numbering has clearly shifted during drafting.
- ✅ **[LOW] Ownership/title language matches the companion Master Lease Agreement** (ref: Article 2.1; Article 1.2 (final interpretation bullet); Article 12.4; Article 1.1 ("Master Lease Agreement", "Vehicle"))
  Title/ownership language is internally consistent and correctly subordinated to the Master Lease Agreement. Article 2.1 affirms 'Wheels shall at all times remain the lessor and legal owner of each Vehicle' and that nothing in the MSA transfers title to the Client. Article 1.2's conflict rule correctly provides that the MLA prevails on 'leasing, title, or return of a Vehicle' while the MSA prevails on 'calculation and billing of Services.' Article 12.4 confirms terminating the MSA does not terminate the MLA. This is the correct fleet-management structure keeping title/lease in the MLA and services in the MSA. One residual dependency: the definitions of 'Vehicle', 'Fleet', 'Vehicle Schedule', 'Rate Class', and the insurance rate-class table (Schedule C) all reference the MLA, so this 'pass' is contingent on the executed MLA actually containing matching title, Rate Class (Schedule C), and Vehicle Schedule provisions.
  → *Recommendation:* None required on the face of the MSA, but confirm at execution that the companion MLA (WMLA reference) in fact uses the identical 'Wheels as lessor and legal owner' formulation, contains the Schedule C Rate Class A-D definitions cited here, and that Vehicle Schedule terminology aligns verbatim so the cross-document definitions do not become orphaned.

## Onboarding Operations
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Onboarding SLA (business days to system setup) is achievable for a manual PDF/email intake process** (ref: Article 9.2 (Service Levels - Onboarding); Article 4.2; Schedule C, Steps 3-5)
  Article 9.2 commits Wheels to complete billing system setup for a Vehicle within ten (10) business days of receipt of complete and accurate executed documentation. The underlying process (Schedule C, Steps 3-5) is entirely manual: the Commercial Team must email the executed PDF to Finance & Billing, who then manually review 16-20 pages to confirm rate class, mileage allowance, elected Services and fees, and then hand-key each Client and Vehicle into the billing system. Ten business days is generally workable for a small enrollment, but the SLA is defined per Vehicle with no carve-out for large or bulk fleet loads (e.g. a 200-vehicle initial onboarding all delivered at once). A single manual reviewer keying hundreds of vehicles cannot reliably hit 10 business days, and there is no batching, volume tier, or 'per batch of N vehicles' language. The 'complete and accurate' precondition (reinforced by Article 4.3) does give Wheels some protection, since the clock only starts on clean documentation, but the clause does not state that the clock pauses/restarts when clarifications are requested.
  → *Recommendation:* Add a volume-based tier to the Article 9.2 onboarding SLA, e.g. 10 business days for enrollments up to a stated vehicle count (say 25 vehicles) per submission, with an additional defined increment (e.g. +5 business days per additional 50 vehicles) for bulk loads. Explicitly state that the SLA clock starts only on receipt of complete and accurate documentation and is tolled during any period Wheels is awaiting Client responses to Article 4.3 clarification requests. This aligns the commitment with realistic manual keying throughput and protects onboarding from being deemed in breach on large initial fleet loads.
- ❌ **[HIGH] No gap where services (e.g. fuel card) could be active before billing setup is complete** (ref: Article 4.1; Article 4.2; Article 5.1; Schedule C, Steps 5-6)
  There is a real onboarding gap risk between physical service activation and billing configuration. Article 4.1 states a Vehicle must not be 'enrolled for billing' before documents are executed, but the sequence in Schedule C only sets up billing at Step 5 and generates the first invoice at Step 6 on the next Billing Cycle. Nothing in the contract ties the issuance/activation of the physical service mechanisms - the fuel card (5.1), toll transponder (5.3), or telematics device (5.4) - to completion of billing setup. In fleet operations, fuel cards and transponders are frequently issued/activated by the operations or card-provider side as soon as the Vehicle is delivered under the Master Lease, which can precede Finance & Billing's manual review and system configuration. If a driver fuels or drives through a toll before Step 5 completes, real pass-through cost (actual fuel and toll charges settled directly by Wheels with third parties) is incurred by Wheels with no billing record set up to capture and invoice it, and the 90-day adjustment window under Article 6.4 could lapse on the earliest transactions. This is a leakage/unbilled-usage exposure, not just a timing nicety.
  → *Recommendation:* Insert an explicit sequencing control stating that no fuel card, toll transponder, or telematics device shall be issued or activated for a Vehicle until that Vehicle's billing setup (Schedule C, Step 5) is confirmed complete, OR, if operations require earlier device activation on delivery, require that a provisional billing record be created at activation so all pass-through usage is captured from day one. Also cross-reference Article 6.4 so the 90-day adjustment window is measured from setup completion, not from the date of the earliest untracked transaction, to eliminate the unbilled-usage gap.
- ✅ **[LOW] Document submission process matches how Finance & Billing actually operates today** (ref: Article 4.1; Article 4.2; Schedule C, Steps 1-6)
  The documented process accurately reflects the current manual, email-based intake workflow. Article 4.1 requires executed documents (16-20 pages) delivered in PDF via email; Article 4.2 describes the Commercial Team forwarding to Finance & Billing for manual review, confirmation of rate class/mileage/Services/fees, and manual system setup; and Schedule C lays out the same six steps with responsible parties (Commercial Team forward via secure email, Finance & Billing manual review and keying). This is consistent with how Finance & Billing operates today and correctly names the Finance & Billing Team, the manual review step, and the next-Billing-Cycle first invoice. The description is faithful; the only minor exposures are operational efficiency ones already captured under the other two items (scale of manual keying and activation sequencing).
  → *Recommendation:* None required for accuracy. As an optional operational hardening step, note internally that the process depends on a single manual handoff (Commercial to Finance & Billing via email); consider adding an internal acknowledgement/receipt checkpoint between Schedule C Step 3 and Step 4 so a forwarded package is never lost in an inbox and the Article 9.2 clock is demonstrably tracked, but this does not require a contract change.

## Service Calculations
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Fuel pass-through is tied to actual pump price with a named reconciliation data source** (ref: Article 5.1, Schedule A, Article 6.4)
  The Fuel Program correctly ties the pass-through to 'actual cost of fuel purchased' and 'Price per Unit at Point of Sale,' which is the correct pump-price basis and avoids rack/index mark-up games. Article 6.4 also states charges are 'based on actual usage data received by Wheels from fuel networks.' However, the specific reconciliation data source is never NAMED. It says generically 'fuel networks' but does not identify the fuel card issuer/network (e.g., WEX, Voyager, Comdata) or reference a settlement/transaction-detail file as the authoritative record against which the Client can audit pump price. Without a named source and a right to the transaction-level detail file, the Client cannot verify that the passed-through price equals the actual POS price, and cannot detect network-level rebates/discounts that Wheels may retain.
  → *Recommendation:* Amend Article 5.1 / Schedule A to name the fuel card network(s) (e.g., WEX/Voyager) and state that the authoritative reconciliation source is the network's transaction-level settlement file, and add a clause requiring that any network volume rebates, discounts, or negotiated pricing be passed through to (or disclosed to) the Client rather than retained by Wheels, since Section 7.1 promises pass-through costs are 'not marked up.'
- ❌ **[HIGH] Mileage allowance is realistic for the fleet's vehicle class/use case, not a generic default** (ref: Article 5.2, Schedule A (Base mileage allowance))
  The contract applies a hard-coded generic default of 15,000 miles/annum 'unless otherwise specified' in a Vehicle Schedule. 15,000 mi/yr is a passenger-car/personal-use figure; it is unrealistic for many commercial fleet use cases (sales fleets routinely run 20,000-30,000+ mi/yr; service/van fleets even higher). At USD 0.12/excess mile, a generic 15,000 default that is not right-sized to the actual duty cycle will systematically generate large excess-mileage charges — the Schedule B example itself shows a Vehicle at 1,450 mi/month (17,400 annualized) already 200 miles/month over allowance. If the Client's fleet is high-mileage and the Vehicle Schedules simply inherit the default, this is a material recurring overcharge risk.
  → *Recommendation:* Do not accept the 15,000-mile default at the MSA level for a commercial fleet. Require each Vehicle Schedule to state an allowance derived from the actual expected annual mileage of that vehicle's duty cycle (sales, service, executive, etc.), and confirm before execution that no Vehicle Schedule is left on the default. Consider a fleet-blended allowance or mileage bands, and negotiate an excess rate and an under-mileage credit that reflect the true residual-value impact rather than a flat USD 0.12.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Mileage true-up mechanic (monthly vs cumulative) is unambiguous and matches billing system capability** (ref: Article 5.2, Schedule A (Excess mileage charge 'Monthly true-up / Lease-end reconciliation'), Schedule B)
  The mileage true-up mechanic is internally contradictory. Article 5.2's Standard Calculation bills excess monthly using a 1/12 monthly allowance (Annual Allowance / 12), and Schedule B demonstrates a monthly excess charge (200 mi over the 1,250 monthly allowance). But the very next paragraph says unused allowance does NOT carry forward month to month, YET excess is 'reconciled across the full Lease Term at Lease-end... by reference to the cumulative allowance... less amounts already invoiced.' These are two different methods that produce different results: monthly billing without carry-forward over-charges a vehicle that has a heavy month and light months, while the cumulative lease-end method nets them out. The clause tries to run both, so the Client can be billed monthly for excess that a cumulative view would erase, and it is unclear whether the lease-end reconciliation refunds those monthly over-bills. Schedule A's 'Monthly true-up / Lease-end reconciliation' label restates the ambiguity rather than resolving it. This also raises the question of whether Wheels' billing system actually performs the negative/refund adjustment at lease-end.
  → *Recommendation:* Pick one primary mechanic and make the other explicitly reconciling. Recommended: bill monthly on a 1/12 basis for cash-flow, but state expressly that at Lease-end a cumulative reconciliation is performed (cumulative actual miles vs cumulative allowance over the elapsed term) and that any net over-billed excess is REFUNDED/credited to the Client, not just used to add charges. Confirm in writing that the Wheels billing system can generate the negative lease-end adjustment; if it cannot, switch to pure cumulative billing. Align the Schedule A label to the chosen mechanic.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Toll liability split for failed transponder reads is fair and enforceable** (ref: Article 5.3 (final paragraph))
  The split assigns all toll violations, administrative penalties, and late fees from a failed transponder read to the Client 'except to the extent such failure is caused by a defect in the transponder attributable to Wheels.' This is skewed toward Wheels. In practice, failed reads are frequently caused by the device/mounting or by Wheels' account administration (e.g., an unregistered plate, a lapsed transponder account, a device Wheels failed to replace within the Article 9.2 ten-business-day SLA), not by driver behavior. The carve-out is narrow ('defect attributable to Wheels') and puts the burden on the Client to prove Wheels' fault for a device the Client neither installs nor controls. It also does not address penalties arising from Wheels' own late settlement with the toll authority.
  → *Recommendation:* Broaden the carve-out so the Client is not liable for violations/penalties/late fees arising from (a) transponder malfunction or misread, (b) Wheels' failure to timely replace a reported malfunctioning device (tie to the 10-business-day SLA in Art. 9.2), (c) Wheels' account/plate registration or late settlement with the toll authority. Client should bear only tolls/penalties caused by driver misuse, an unreported lost/removed device, or operating a non-enrolled lane. Also require Wheels to pass the underlying toll authority documentation so fault can be determined objectively.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Telematics pro-ration method matches how the billing system actually pro-rates** (ref: Article 5.4, Schedule A, Schedule B (Telematics worked example))
  The pro-ration method is stated consistently as a fixed 1/30th-per-day basis across Article 5.4, Schedule A, and the Schedule B worked example (18 days x USD 14.00/30 = USD 8.40), so the method is internally consistent and unambiguous on its face. The flag is that a fixed 1/30 daily divisor does not match actual calendar days for 28-, 29-, and 31-day months: a vehicle enrolled all 31 days of a 31-day month would compute to 31/30 x USD 14.00 = USD 14.47 (over-billing beyond the full monthly rate) unless the system caps at the full package rate, and February enrollments are under/over stated. The contract does not state that the daily charge is capped at the full monthly package rate, nor confirm the billing system uses a 1/30 convention rather than actual-days. This is a known source of telematics pro-ration disputes.
  → *Recommendation:* Add an explicit cap stating the pro-rated charge shall never exceed the full monthly package rate, and clarify whether partial-month enrollment counts the enrollment and de-enrollment days. Confirm with Wheels' billing operations that the system actually uses the 1/30 convention (not actual calendar days); if the system pro-rates on actual days, correct Article 5.4/Schedule A/B to match so the contract mirrors system behavior.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Insurance Rate Classes referenced are actually defined in a schedule that exists** (ref: Article 1.1 (Rate Class def.), Article 5.5, Schedule A note, Schedule B (Insurance example))
  Every reference to the Insurance Program premium depends on Rate Classes A-D that are stated to be 'defined in Schedule C to the Master Lease Agreement' — a document OUTSIDE this Agreement and not attached here. This MSA has its own Schedules A, B, and C, and its Schedule C is the Onboarding process, NOT a rate-class definition — creating a naming collision and a real risk of cross-reference error. The actual per-class premium dollar amounts are not in Schedule A (it just says 'Per Vehicle rate per Schedule, by rate class'); the only concrete number (Class B = USD 96.00) appears solely in the illustrative Schedule B example, which expressly states it does not set definitive rates. So there is no verifiable, in-force schedule within the reviewed documents that actually defines the Rate Classes or their premiums, and the Rate Class definition in Art. 1.1 also hedges with 'or as otherwise defined.'
  → *Recommendation:* Before execution, obtain and confirm that Schedule C to the Master Lease Agreement exists, is executed, and actually defines Classes A-D with the assignment criteria AND the per-class premium dollar amounts. Either incorporate the per-class premium table directly into this MSA's Schedule A or add a firm cross-reference with the MLA Schedule C version/date. Rename references to avoid confusion with this MSA's own Schedule C. Do not rely on the USD 96.00 figure, which is illustrative only.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] No double-billing risk between overlapping services (e.g. insurance premium vs waiver)** (ref: Article 5.5, Article 11.3, Schedule A/B (Insurance), Article 5.2/5.4 (mileage vs telematics))
  Two overlap risks. (1) Insurance premium vs Physical Damage Waiver: Art. 5.5 bills Rate-Class Premium (which already includes 'physical damage insurance coverage') PLUS a separate USD 35.00/mo Physical Damage Waiver. These are conceptually different (the premium is Wheels' insurance; the Waiver waives Wheels' subrogation/recovery against the Client above the deductible), but the contract never explains why a Client already paying for physical damage coverage in the premium must also pay a monthly waiver, nor how the deductible interacts — a classic double-charge perception and potential real overlap for physical-damage risk. (2) Mileage verification via telematics: Art. 5.2 and 5.4 both bill for mileage/odometer data (a USD 3.50/mo Mileage Log & Reporting Fee AND a USD 14/22 telematics package that also 'supports mileage verification'), so a Client on both may be paying twice for the same odometer-capture function.
  → *Recommendation:* For insurance: add a sentence clarifying the distinct risk each charge covers (premium = third-party/first-party insurance; Waiver = relief from Client's own out-of-pocket physical-damage liability above deductible) and confirm the Waiver is truly optional and not duplicative of coverage already funded by the premium. For mileage/telematics: state that where the Telematics Program supplies odometer data, the USD 3.50 Mileage Log & Reporting Fee is either waived or reduced to avoid charging twice for the same mileage-capture function, or expressly justify the fee as a distinct reporting/compliance service.

## Billing Payment
- ✅ **[LOW] Payment term and late interest are within usury limits of the governing state** (ref: Article 6.2; Article 14.1)
  Article 6.2 sets net-30 payment terms and late interest at the lesser of 1.5% per month (18% per annum) or the maximum rate permitted by applicable law. The built-in 'lesser of ... maximum rate permitted by applicable law' savings clause means the rate can never exceed the governing state's usury ceiling, so this is self-correcting regardless of which state fills the [Governing State] blank. Net-30 is standard for consolidated fleet invoices.
  → *Recommendation:* Substantively fine, but confirm the [Governing State] blank in Article 14.1 is actually populated before execution. In commercial B2B contexts most states exempt or set high ceilings, but a few (e.g., a per-annum cap below 18%) could bite the stated 1.5%/month; the savings clause protects you either way. Consider adding that the reduced lawful rate applies only for the period the cap is exceeded so the 1.5% snaps back if law changes.
- ⚠️ **[HIGH] Dispute window is realistic for a client auditing a multi-vehicle, multi-service invoice** (ref: Article 6.3; Article 6.1; Article 9.2)
  Article 6.3 gives the Client only 15 days from the Invoice date to dispute charges in writing. But the Invoice is a single consolidated statement (Article 6.1) covering Rentals plus five Services broken down by vehicle across the entire Fleet, and Wheels itself has up to 10 business days after Billing Cycle close just to issue the invoice (Article 9.2). A fleet client reconciling per-transaction fuel, per-crossing toll, mileage true-ups and insurance premiums line-by-line against its own telematics/fuel-card data realistically cannot audit a large multi-vehicle invoice within 15 days of the invoice date. This window is short relative to the 90-day reconciliation right Wheels reserves for itself (Article 6.4) and the 30-day support/resolution timelines elsewhere, creating an asymmetry that favors Wheels.
  → *Recommendation:* Extend the dispute window to at least 30 days from invoice receipt (not invoice date) to match fleet audit cycles, and expressly tie the clock to the date the itemized backup data (transaction-level fuel/toll detail) is made available, since disputes can't be substantiated without it. Also confirm the dispute window runs from receipt to avoid mail/transmission delay eroding the period.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Suspension-for-nonpayment clause doesn't inadvertently cut compliance-critical services** (ref: Article 6.6; Article 5.3; Article 5.5)
  Article 6.6 correctly carves out the Insurance Program from suspension while a Vehicle remains in operation, which protects the most compliance-critical service. However, it permits Wheels to suspend 'one or more Services' otherwise at its discretion, which would include the Toll Program and the Mileage & Logging Program. Suspending the Toll Program mid-operation exposes vehicles to toll violations, administrative penalties and late fees (which under Article 5.3 the Client bears), and suspending Mileage & Logging cuts the odometer/mileage log that the contract itself says is used for 'tax/compliance reporting purposes' (Article 5.2). These are compliance-adjacent services that suspension could inadvertently disrupt, generating downstream penalties for the Client on unrelated Services even where only one line item is unpaid.
  → *Recommendation:* Narrow the suspension right so it (a) applies only to the specific Service(s) tied to the unpaid amount, not the whole bundle, and (b) additionally excludes the Toll Program and Mileage & Logging Program from suspension while vehicles are in operation, or at minimum deactivates toll transponders in a way that prevents violation exposure. Also require that suspension not trigger Client liability for penalties (e.g., toll violations under 5.3) that arise from the suspension itself.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Reconciliation cap aligns with how long fuel/toll data actually takes to settle** (ref: Article 6.4)
  Article 6.4 caps retroactive adjustments at 90 days after the original usage occurred (except fraud/manifest error). For fuel-card data this is comfortable, and for most electronic toll transactions 90 days is workable. However, toll authority settlement in practice can run longer than 90 days for certain agencies, pay-by-plate/video-toll crossings, out-of-network/interoperable toll reciprocity, and delayed authority billing, meaning legitimately late-arriving toll charges could fall outside the window and become non-billable (or force Wheels to rely on the 'manifest error' exception, which is a poor fit for merely-late data). The single 90-day cap treats fuel and toll uniformly despite their different settlement timelines.
  → *Recommendation:* Retain 90 days for fuel and mileage, but extend the reconciliation window for the Toll Program to align with actual toll-authority settlement (commonly 120-180 days for video/interoperable tolls), or add a carve-out for 'late-arriving toll authority charges' distinct from the fraud/manifest-error exception. As the client's analyst, note that a longer toll window cuts both ways (Wheels can also bill you later), so pair any extension with a requirement that adjustments be supported by underlying toll-authority documentation.

## Fees Adjustments
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] CPI-linked escalation explicitly excludes market-driven pass-through costs (fuel, toll, insurance)** (ref: Article 7.2 (Annual Adjustment); Article 7.1; Schedule A; Article 5.5 (Insurance) / Article 8.3 referenced therein)
  The CPI/3% annual escalation cap in Article 7.2 is correctly drafted to reach only the 'fixed-fee components of the Services set out in Schedule A (excluding pass-through costs).' Fuel pump price and toll authority charges are expressly carved out as un-marked-up pass-throughs (Art. 7.1 and Schedule A), so the CPI cap does not touch them - that part passes. HOWEVER, the exclusion is incomplete on the insurance side. Insurance premiums are NOT a fixed Schedule A fee; they are Rate-Class premiums that Article 5.5 lets Wheels re-price once every 12 months for 'claims experience, insurance market conditions, or vehicle risk classification' with NO 3%/CPI ceiling. So the largest market-driven cost line escalates outside the CPI cap through a separate uncapped mechanism, and the drafting never states that insurance is excluded from Art. 7.2 - it simply lives in a parallel, uncapped clause. There is also a broken cross-reference: Art. 5.5 points to 'Article 8.3' for notice, but Article 8 contains no 8.3 (notice for premium changes is effectively undefined). Net effect: from the Client's perspective the CPI cap gives false comfort - it caps admin/logging/transponder/telematics fees (the small dollars) while fuel, toll AND insurance (the large dollars) all move on uncapped, market-driven mechanisms.
  → *Recommendation:* Add an express statement in Art. 7.2 that CPI/3% escalation applies only to fixed administrative fee components and does NOT apply to (and does not cap) fuel pump price, toll authority charges, or Rate-Class insurance premiums, which move per Art. 5.1/5.3/5.5. Separately, for insurance - the one pass-through the Client can actually negotiate - put guardrails on Art. 5.5: cap annual Rate-Class premium increases (e.g., to loss-ratio-justified changes with supporting claims data), require 60-90 days' notice, and give the Client a right to obtain replacement coverage under Art. 12.3 if an increase exceeds a threshold. Fix the dead cross-reference (Art. 5.5 'Article 8.3' should point to a real notice clause, e.g., Art. 7.2/7.3 notice periods).
- ⚠️ **[HIGH] Extraordinary adjustment / service-termination right is operationally workable against the MLA** (ref: Article 7.3 (Extraordinary Adjustments); Article 12.3 (Termination of Individual Services); Article 6.6; Article 5.5; MLA (companion WMLA))
  Article 7.3 gives Wheels a 90-day-notice extraordinary rate increase for material, sustained cost changes (fuel disruption, insurance hardening), and the Client's only remedy is to terminate the affected Service under Art. 12.3 before the increase takes effect. The mechanics look symmetrical but are operationally unworkable against the Master Lease Agreement in the two cases that actually trigger 7.3: (1) INSURANCE - if Wheels raises insurance under an insurance-market-hardening event, the Client 'terminates' the Insurance Program under Art. 12.3, but Art. 12.3 conditions insurance discontinuation on the Client demonstrating replacement coverage meeting the MLA minimums. During a genuine hardening market, replacement fleet coverage is exactly what is scarce/expensive - so the escape hatch is illusory precisely when it is needed, and the Client is realistically forced to accept the increase or breach the MLA. (2) FUEL - the Client cannot practically operate leased Vehicles under the MLA with no fuel mechanism, so 'terminating the Fuel Program' is not a real economic choice either; it just removes the fuel card while the lease obligation continues. Timing is also mismatched: Art. 7.3 gives 90 days' notice but Art. 12.3 requires 30 days' notice to terminate, and there is no stated ability to terminate the whole MSA (or exit the underlying lease) if the extraordinary increase is unacceptable - Art. 12.4 confirms MSA termination does NOT terminate the MLA. So the Client can shed a Service but stays locked into a 36-month lease whose economics depend on that Service. There is no materiality threshold, no cap, no requirement that Wheels evidence the cost change, and no reciprocal price-down when the extraordinary condition abates.
  → *Recommendation:* Make the termination remedy real against the MLA: (a) for insurance-driven Art. 7.3 increases, give the Client a right to reject the increase and require Wheels to either hold the prior rate or allow the Client a commercially reasonable cure window (e.g., 60-90 days) to source replacement coverage without being in MLA breach, and expressly suspend the MLA insurance-minimum default during that window; (b) add a quantitative materiality trigger (e.g., extraordinary adjustment only where documented third-party cost rises >X%) and require Wheels to provide supporting cost evidence with the notice; (c) add a cap or good-faith negotiation step, and a symmetrical roll-back once the fuel/insurance disruption subsides; (d) align the notice periods (make the Art. 12.3 termination window run from, and be no shorter than, the Art. 7.3 notice, so the Client can actually opt out before the increase bites); and (e) for increases above a threshold affecting fuel or insurance, give the Client a right to terminate the affected Vehicle(s) at the MSA level with a coordinated early-termination path under the MLA, rather than being stripped of the Service while remaining on-lease.

## Liability Data
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Liability cap scope (what's excluded, e.g. Rentals) is intentional and doesn't create a coverage gap** (ref: Article 11.2; Article 11.3; Article 1.1 (definition of 'Invoice'/'Rentals'); Article 6.1)
  The liability cap is set at 'the total fees paid or payable by the Client under this Agreement (EXCLUDING RENTALS UNDER THE MASTER LEASE AGREEMENT) during the twelve months preceding the event.' Excluding Rentals from the cap-base is intentional and correct in principle: Rentals are consideration for the lease of the vehicle (title/financing), not for the fleet management Services this MSA actually governs, so folding them into a Services liability cap would arbitrarily inflate the cap. HOWEVER two real coverage gaps arise from this construction. (1) The cap is measured only against MSA Service fees, which for a fleet are small per-vehicle amounts (e.g. USD 3.50 mileage log fee, USD 14-22 telematics, USD 0.15/0.45 transaction fees). For a fleet where the dominant dollar exposure is fuel and toll PASS-THROUGH cost and insurance premium, a cap pegged to thin administration fees can be a fraction of the harm a Wheels billing/telematics error could actually cause the Client (e.g. systemic fuel-fraud that goes undetected, or mis-reported IFTA/mileage data creating tax exposure). (2) Because the MSA and MLA are deliberately bifurcated (Art. 1.2, 2.1), a loss that straddles both documents — e.g. a telematics/odometer data error that simultaneously drives an incorrect excess-mileage/Lease-end reconciliation charge under the MLA — could fall into a seam where the MSA cap applies but the MLA has its own (possibly higher or lower) regime, and neither instrument clearly governs. The exclusion of Rentals is defensible; the gap is that the remaining cap base is unusually thin relative to fleet-scale operational exposure.
  → *Recommendation:* Keep the Rentals exclusion but (a) reset the cap floor: express the cap as the greater of trailing-12-month Service fees OR a fixed dollar minimum (e.g. USD 250k-500k, or a stated multiple of annual Service fees) so a genuine billing/telematics failure across a large fleet isn't capped at a few thousand dollars of admin fees; (b) confirm whether pass-through fuel/toll/insurance amounts count toward 'fees paid... under this Agreement' — if the intent is Service (margin) fees only, say so, and separately confirm that Wheels bears full liability for erroneously passed-through third-party charges outside the cap since those are actual Client money, not Wheels' margin; (c) add a bridging clause stating that where a single event gives rise to claims under both the MSA and MLA, the caps are not cumulatively re-triggered and identifying which instrument's cap controls, to close the MSA/MLA seam.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Confidentiality/data clauses satisfy telematics/GPS privacy obligations in the operating jurisdiction** (ref: Article 10.1, 10.2, 10.3; Article 5.4; Article 14.1 (Governing State blank))
  The data provisions are materially thin for a telematics/GPS fleet product and do not, as drafted, satisfy the privacy obligations that attach to continuous location tracking and driver-behavior monitoring. Specific deficiencies: (1) DRIVER PRIVACY, NOT JUST CLIENT DATA. Art. 10 treats data as Client 'Confidential Information' and Wheels' use restriction, but telematics collects PERSONAL DATA about individual drivers (precise geolocation, driving behavior, potentially off-hours movement). The Agreement is silent on driver notice/consent, and allocates no responsibility for obtaining it. Several states impose specific rules: California (CCPA/CPRA — precise geolocation is 'sensitive personal information'; and Cal. Labor Code / vehicle-tracking notice rules), plus emerging state privacy laws (CO, CT, VA, TX, etc.) and, where drivers are unionized or in certain states, statutory notice before GPS tracking of employees. (2) NO CONTROLLER/PROCESSOR (or business/service-provider) ROLE ALLOCATION and no data-processing addendum — a baseline expectation for a product built on location data. (3) NO BREACH NOTIFICATION obligation or timeline anywhere, despite Art. 10.3 acknowledging safeguards and Art. 4 sending executed contracts (containing PII) over ordinary email. (4) NO DATA RETENTION / DELETION / RETURN-ON-TERMINATION provision for telematics data — Art. 12.4 addresses payment but not data. (5) Art. 10.2's carve-out ('shall not SELL such data to unaffiliated third parties for their independent marketing purposes') is narrower than it appears — it does not restrict sharing with AFFILIATES, or disclosure/use for non-marketing secondary purposes, or 'sharing' for cross-context behavioral advertising (a distinct regulated category under CPRA). (6) The governing state is left blank (Art. 14.1), so the very jurisdiction whose privacy statute must be satisfied is undetermined — the clause cannot be validated until it is filled.
  → *Recommendation:* Add a dedicated Telematics/Driver Data section (or DPA addendum) that: (a) allocates responsibility for driver notice and any required consent — for a fleet product the market norm is that the Client (as employer) provides notice/consent to drivers and warrants it has done so, with Wheels processing only on documented instructions; (b) designates roles (Client = controller/business re: driver data; Wheels = processor/service provider) and prohibits Wheels from retaining, using, or disclosing the data outside providing the Services and legal compliance — mirroring CPRA service-provider contract language so Wheels' receipt is not a 'sale'/'share'; (c) inserts a breach-notification obligation with a concrete outbound timeline (e.g. notify Client without undue delay and within 72 hours) and cooperation on statutory breach notices; (d) adds retention limits and a data return/deletion right on termination (tie to Art. 12.4); (e) tighten Art. 10.2 to cover affiliate sharing and secondary uses, and expressly cover 'sharing' for cross-context behavioral advertising; (f) require encrypted/secure transmission for the Art. 4 executed-document email flow rather than ordinary email; (g) once the Governing State is chosen, run a state-specific check (esp. CA, IL BIPA if any biometric driver-monitoring, and any state employee GPS-notice statute) and add the corresponding compliance rep.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Insurance claims carve-out from the liability cap is legally sound in the governing state** (ref: Article 11.3; Article 11.2; Article 5.5; Article 6.6; Article 14.1 (Governing State blank))
  Art. 11.3 ('Insurance Program Not a Limitation') is directionally sound: it sensibly clarifies that the inter-party Article 11 cap does not reduce coverage/benefits actually payable under the Insurance Program, and distinguishes claims 'between the Parties arising under this Agreement' from 'first- or third-party insurance claims properly payable under the Insurance Program.' Preserving insurance proceeds from a contractual liability cap is standard and enforceable. But the carve-out's soundness cannot be confirmed as drafted because: (1) GOVERNING STATE IS BLANK (Art. 14.1). The enforceability of liability limitations and, more importantly, the effect of any anti-subrogation / anti-waiver / financial-responsibility rules on vehicle insurance is state-specific; the carve-out cannot be validated in a vacuum. (2) STRUCTURAL AMBIGUITY re: who the insurer is. Art. 5.5 says Wheels 'shall arrange and maintain' coverage 'consistent with Wheels' standard fleet insurance program' — this could mean third-party carrier insurance, a Wheels self-insured/captive arrangement, or a Physical Damage Waiver (which is a contractual waiver, NOT insurance). The carve-out lumps 'first- or third-party insurance claims' together without clarifying that the Physical Damage Waiver is a contractual, not insurance, benefit — in some states (e.g. those regulating rental-vehicle 'damage waivers') mischaracterizing a waiver as insurance raises regulatory/enforceability issues. (3) OVERLAP WITH GROSS NEGLIGENCE EXCLUSIONS. The PDW is 'subject to the exclusions... (including gross negligence, willful misconduct, and unauthorized use)' in the MLA, while Art. 11.2 makes gross negligence/willful misconduct an UNCAPPED head of liability — the interaction (where an excluded-from-waiver loss also = uncapped MSA liability) is not reconciled.
  → *Recommendation:* (a) Populate Art. 14.1 and then confirm the carve-out against that state's law on liability-limitation enforceability, anti-subrogation, and motor-vehicle financial-responsibility requirements; (b) clarify in Art. 5.5/11.3 the actual insurance structure — name whether coverage is placed with a licensed third-party carrier vs. Wheels self-insurance/captive — because a self-insured Wheels 'insurance claim' is functionally a claim against Wheels and a court could collapse it back into the Art. 11 cap unless clearly ring-fenced; (c) expressly state that the Physical Damage Waiver is a contractual waiver and not insurance, and confirm compliance with any state damage-waiver disclosure/regulation; (d) reconcile Art. 11.3 with Art. 11.2 by confirming that amounts payable under the Insurance Program are additive to (not offset against) any uncapped gross-negligence/willful-misconduct liability, and address the scenario where a PDW-excluded loss (gross negligence) simultaneously triggers uncapped MSA liability so there is no double recovery or unintended gap.

## Termination Boilerplate
- ✅ **[MEDIUM] Termination of the MSA vs MLA interaction is intentional, not an oversight** (ref: Article 12.4; Article 3.3; Article 1.2 (conflict rule); Article 2.1)
  The MSA deliberately decouples itself from the companion Master Lease Agreement. Article 12.4 expressly states that termination of the MSA 'shall not, by itself, terminate the Master Lease Agreement, which shall continue to be governed by its own terms.' This is reinforced by the conflict-of-order rules in Article 1.2 and the companion-agreements architecture in Article 2.1, and by the survival of Article 12.4 under Article 3.3. The decoupling is clearly intentional, and it is the commercially correct default for a fleet lessor: the lease (and Wheels' ownership of and residual interest in the vehicles) must survive even if the ancillary fuel/toll/telematics/insurance services stop. However, the drafting is one-directional. It addresses what happens to the MLA if the MSA is terminated, but is silent on the reverse scenario: whether termination of the MLA (e.g., for lease default, vehicle return, or insolvency) automatically terminates or hollows out the MSA. Because Services under this MSA can only exist in respect of Vehicles leased under the MLA (see the definitions of 'Fleet', 'Vehicle', and 'Service'), a terminated MLA would leave an MSA with no vehicles to service but no clean exit mechanism.
  → *Recommendation:* Retain the intentional survival of the MLA in Article 12.4, but add a reciprocal provision addressing the downstream direction: state that if the MLA terminates in full (or a Vehicle is returned/repossessed), the MSA (or the Services for the affected Vehicles) automatically terminates as to those Vehicles, with a final reconciliation of pass-through and Lease-end excess-mileage charges under Articles 5.2 and 6.4. Also confirm the intended treatment of the Insurance Program on MSA termination while a Vehicle remains leased, to avoid a coverage gap (cross-reference Articles 6.6 and 12.3, which already prohibit suspending/discontinuing insurance on an operating leased Vehicle without replacement coverage).
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Assignment clause balances Wheels' M&A flexibility against client counterparty risk** (ref: Article 16.3)
  Article 16.3 gives Wheels a broad consent-free assignment right to an affiliate or 'in connection with a merger, acquisition, or sale of substantially all of its assets relating to this Agreement,' while the Client needs prior written consent to assign. This appropriately preserves Wheels' M&A and internal-reorganization flexibility, which is essential for a fleet lessor whose lease/service book is a frequently traded asset. The clause is not fully balanced against Client counterparty risk, however. Three gaps: (1) The affiliate carve-out is open-ended and does not require the affiliate to have the financial and operational capacity to perform the fuel-card float, toll settlement, and insurance obligations, nor a snap-back if the assignee ceases to be an affiliate. (2) On an M&A assignment, the clause requires the assignee to assume Wheels' obligations, but gives the Client no protection where the assignee is a competitor, an uncreditworthy entity, or a party that cannot maintain the insurance program the Client relies on. (3) Because the MSA is tightly bound to the MLA and to Wheels' vehicle ownership, a change of control of Wheels engages more Client counterparty risk than the clause acknowledges (continuity of insurance coverage, telematics data handling under Article 10, and pass-through payment mechanics).
  → *Recommendation:* Keep Wheels' consent-free affiliate and M&A assignment right, but condition it on Client-protective guardrails suited to fleet management: (a) require the assignee to be capable of maintaining the Insurance Program coverage and to assume all Article 10 data-protection obligations for telematics/location data; (b) add a light-touch notice-and-continuity obligation (written notice within X days of any change of control or assignment) and a snap-back requiring re-assignment if the affiliate ceases to be an affiliate; and (c) consider carving the Client the right to consent (not to be unreasonably withheld) where the proposed assignee is a direct competitor of the Client or materially less creditworthy than Wheels.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Arbitration seat/institution placeholders are flagged as required-before-execution** (ref: Article 14.3; Article 14.1)
  Article 14.3 contains two unresolved, execution-critical open items: the arbitral institution is left as 'a mutually agreed arbitral institution' (no institution actually named) and the seat is a bracketed placeholder '[City, State].' Article 14.1 similarly leaves the governing law as '[Governing State].' An agreement to arbitrate that defers both the administering institution and the seat to future mutual agreement risks being unenforceable or, at minimum, forcing costly satellite litigation to constitute the tribunal precisely when the parties are already in dispute. For a fleet-management book that Wheels replicates across many clients and multiple states, an undefined seat/institution also undermines Wheels' ability to consolidate or predict its dispute forum. These placeholders must be resolved before signature, not left to post-execution negotiation.
  → *Recommendation:* Before execution, hard-code (a) a named administering institution and its rules (e.g., AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules or JAMS), (b) a specific seat/venue '[City, State],' and (c) the '[Governing State]' in Article 14.1, ideally aligning the governing law, seat, and Wheels' principal operating jurisdiction for consistency across Wheels' standard-form MSAs. Add these three fields to the pre-signature checklist as blocking items, and confirm consistency with any arbitration/venue clause in the companion Master Lease Agreement to avoid split-forum disputes between the MSA and MLA.

## Service Calculations

- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Fuel pass-through is tied to actual pump price with a named reconciliation data source** (ref: Art. 5.1; Sch. A (Fuel Program); Art. 6.4)
  Art. 5.1 and Schedule A tie the fuel charge to 'actual cost of fuel purchased' / 'actual cost per gallon/litre at point of sale,' which correctly anchors to pump price and confirms Wheels pays the network directly with only a USD 0.15/transaction admin fee (no markup per Art. 7.1). However, the reconciliation source is described only generically as 'usage data received by Wheels from fuel networks' (Art. 6.4). No named fuel card processor / network settlement file (e.g., WEX, Voyager, Comdata) is identified as the authoritative reconciliation dataset, and there is no mechanism entitling the Client to the underlying transaction-level detail (station, date, product grade, volume, unit price) to verify that billed 'point of sale' price equals the actual pump price. This creates a verification gap: 'point of sale' price could diverge from posted pump price where network fees or price-per-gallon rounding are embedded.
  → _Recommendation:_ Name the specific fuel card network/processor and the settlement file as the authoritative reconciliation source in Art. 5.1 or Schedule A, and add a Client right to receive transaction-level fuel detail (station ID, date/time, product grade, gross gallons, unit price, tax) supporting each Billing Cycle. State expressly that the billed unit price equals the network-settled pump price with no spread, and confirm the 90-day adjustment window in Art. 6.4 applies to fuel price corrections.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Mileage allowance is realistic for the fleet's vehicle class/use case, not a generic default** (ref: Art. 5.2; Sch. A (Base mileage allowance))
  The allowance is set at the generic 15,000 miles/annum default (Art. 5.2 and Schedule A) 'unless otherwise specified' in a Vehicle Schedule. 15,000 mi/yr is a passenger/light-duty personal-use benchmark; it is materially low for commercial use cases such as sales fleets, service vans, or delivery vehicles (often 20,000-35,000+ mi/yr). Because the Vehicle Schedule is a placeholder and no fleet-specific profile is attached, the contract as drafted defaults every Vehicle to a personal-use number. At USD 0.12/mile excess (Schedule A), a high-mileage commercial fleet defaulted to 15,000 could accrue substantial and predictable excess charges purely from a mis-set default.
  → _Recommendation:_ Do not rely on the 15,000-mile default. Confirm each Vehicle Schedule specifies a class-appropriate allowance derived from the Client's actual duty cycle (e.g., 25,000-30,000 for sales/service fleets). Consider adding a fleet-average pooling option so under-utilized Vehicles offset high-mileage ones, and confirm the USD 0.12/mile excess rate is benchmarked to residual-value impact for the specific vehicle class rather than applied as a flat default.
- ⚠️ **[HIGH] Mileage true-up mechanic (monthly vs cumulative) is unambiguous and matches billing system capability** (ref: Art. 5.2; Sch. A; Sch. B (Mileage worked example))
  There is an internal contradiction in the true-up mechanic. Art. 5.2's Standard Calculation and the Schedule B worked example bill excess mileage MONTHLY on a 1/12-of-allowance basis (1,450 actual vs 1,250 monthly allowance = 200 excess miles x USD 0.12 = USD 24.00 billed that month). But the following paragraph in Art. 5.2 says unused monthly allowance does NOT carry forward yet excess is 'reconciled across the full Lease Term at Lease-end... by reference to the cumulative allowance... against cumulative actual miles driven, less amounts already invoiced.' A pure monthly no-carry-forward method and a cumulative Lease-end reconciliation produce different totals: a Vehicle that drives 1,450 one month and 1,000 the next is 'excess' monthly but exactly on allowance cumulatively, yet the drafting bills the month-1 excess AND then trues up at Lease-end. Schedule A even labels the row 'Monthly true-up / Lease-end reconciliation,' embedding the ambiguity. It is unclear whether interim monthly excess charges are refundable at Lease-end true-up, and whether the billing system runs monthly-only, cumulative-only, or both.
  → _Recommendation:_ Pick one primary mechanic and make the other reconciling. Recommended: bill an interim monthly estimate but state explicitly that the Lease-end cumulative reconciliation is definitive, that all interim monthly excess charges are credited/netted against the cumulative calculation, and that over-collected interim excess is refunded. Delete the 'does not carry forward' language or reconcile it with the cumulative method. Confirm the billing system can (a) hold interim charges as reversible and (b) run a cumulative Lease-term true-up; align Schedule B's example to the chosen method.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Toll liability split for failed transponder reads is fair and enforceable** (ref: Art. 5.3 (final paragraph))
  Art. 5.3 makes the Client responsible for all toll violations, administrative penalties, and late fees arising from transponder read failures 'except to the extent such failure is caused by a defect in the transponder attributable to Wheels.' The split is directionally defensible (driver/plate-cover/tailgating causes are the Client's; hardware defects are Wheels'). It excludes common Wheels-side non-'defect' causes such as mis-provisioned toll accounts, delayed account activation during onboarding, or Wheels' failure to replace a device within the Art. 9.2 10-business-day SLA. As drafted the Client could bear penalties for a Wheels administrative/SLA failure that is not technically a hardware 'defect.'
  → _Recommendation:_ Broaden the Wheels-side carve-out from 'defect in the transponder' to any read failure caused by Wheels' hardware, account provisioning/activation, or failure to meet the device-replacement SLA in Art. 9.2. Add that once the Client timely reports a malfunction under Art. 8.2/9.2, penalties accruing after the report are Wheels' responsibility, and give the Client access to toll read/exception data to substantiate cause. ## Billing Payment

- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Payment term and late interest are within usury limits of the governing state** (ref: Article 6.2 (read with Article 14.1))
  Net-30 payment terms are standard and unobjectionable for fleet management. The late-interest rate of 1.5% per month (18% per annum) is expressed as 'the lesser of 1.5% per month or the maximum rate permitted by applicable law,' which is a self-limiting savings clause that protects against usury violations. However, the governing state is left blank ('[Governing State]' in Article 14.1), so the actual usury ceiling cannot be verified. 18% APR exceeds the civil/contract usury cap in several states (e.g., New York's 16% general usury cap for certain obligations, or states that cap commercial default interest). While the savings-clause language prevents an outright illegal charge, an above-cap stated rate can still be challenged and, in a few jurisdictions, risks the interest provision being read down or (rarely) the loan-characterization consequences. For a fleet billing relationship this is more a collectability/enforceability question than a criminal-usury one, but it needs confirmation.
  → _Recommendation:_ Fill in the Governing State and confirm 18% APR sits under that state's commercial contract-interest / usury ceiling before execution. If the chosen state caps below 18% (e.g., NY at 16%), either lower the stated monthly rate to match, or rely explicitly on the savings clause and document the applicable cap. Keep the 'lesser of' savings-clause wording — it is the correct protective drafting for a multi-state fleet.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Dispute window is realistic for a client auditing a multi-vehicle, multi-service invoice** (ref: Article 6.3)
  The dispute window is only 15 days from the Invoice date, and the invoice under Article 6.1 is a single consolidated statement covering Rentals plus five Services (fuel, tolls, mileage, telematics, insurance) itemized per Vehicle across an entire fleet. For a multi-vehicle fleet, a client must reconcile hundreds or thousands of individual fuel-card transactions, toll crossings, and odometer entries against driver records to detect misuse, duplicate tolls, or mis-posted transactions. 15 calendar days is unrealistic for that volume of line-item auditing — especially given Wheels itself has up to 10 business days after the Billing Cycle close just to issue the invoice (Article 9.2), further compressing the client's effective review time. This mismatch will cause legitimate disputes (e.g., fuel-fraud flags, toll double-charges) to be time-barred. Note also that the reconciliation window in 6.4 lets Wheels adjust charges for 90 days, creating an asymmetry: Wheels has 90 days to correct, the client has 15 to object.
  → _Recommendation:_ Extend the client dispute window to at least 30 days, and ideally 45–60 days, from the Invoice date to match fleet-audit reality and the 90-day reconciliation window in 6.4. Alternatively, tie the window to a defined number of business days after invoice delivery (not invoice date) so Wheels' own 10-business-day issuance lag doesn't eat into the client's review time. Preserve the 'pay undisputed amounts by Due Date' mechanic — that part is fair.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Suspension-for-nonpayment clause doesn't inadvertently cut compliance-critical services** (ref: Article 6.6 (read with 5.5, 12.3, 8.1))
  The clause correctly carves out the Insurance Program from suspension while a Vehicle remains in operation — a critical protection that prevents an uninsured vehicle on the road. However, it permits suspension of 'one or more Services' without carving out other compliance-critical or safety functions. Suspending the Toll Program mid-relationship can trigger toll-authority violations, administrative penalties, and late fees (for which the Client bears responsibility under 5.3), turning a payment dispute into regulatory citations. Suspending the Telematics Program can cut the data feed that supports mileage/tax-compliance reporting (5.2) and safety/MVR monitoring, and suspending the Mileage & Logging Program can break the tax/compliance mileage log the Agreement itself says is maintained 'for both billing and tax/compliance reporting purposes.' Only insurance is protected; the other compliance-adjacent services are exposed.
  → _Recommendation:_ Broaden the suspension carve-out beyond Insurance to any Service whose interruption creates a legal/regulatory exposure for vehicles still in operation — specifically Toll (to avoid violations the Client is charged for) and the compliance/tax-reporting elements of Mileage & Telematics. At minimum, add language that Wheels will continue passing through and settling toll and insurance obligations during suspension, and that suspension may not create a violation or lapse for which the Client is then held liable under 5.3.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Reconciliation cap aligns with how long fuel/toll data actually takes to settle** (ref: Article 6.4)
  The 90-day reconciliation cap is reasonable for fuel-card data, which typically settles from fuel networks within days to a few weeks. It is adequate for most toll traffic as well. The exposure is toll and violation settlement: some toll authorities (particularly cashless/pay-by-plate and interoperable inter-agency toll networks) can post charges, image-based tolls, and administrative toll notices 60–120+ days after the crossing, occasionally longer for cross-jurisdiction reconciliation. A hard 90-day cap could bar Wheels from passing through late-arriving legitimate toll authority charges — meaning Wheels absorbs them, or (worse) re-bills the client outside the contractual window and triggers a dispute. The fraud/manifest-error exception does not cover ordinary late toll-authority posting. Mileage true-up is separately handled at Lease-end under 5.2, so that is fine.
  → _Recommendation:_ Either extend the reconciliation cap to 120 days specifically for Toll Program pass-throughs (while keeping 90 days for fuel and mileage), or add an express exception for 'late-posted toll authority charges received by Wheels after the reconciliation period,' capped at the date Wheels actually receives the toll-authority data plus a short window. This keeps the client protected against stale billing while aligning the cap with real toll-settlement timelines and preventing surprise absorbed or out-of-window toll charges.

## Fees Adjustments

- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] CPI-linked escalation explicitly excludes market-driven pass-through costs (fuel, toll, insurance)** (ref: Article 7.2 (Annual Adjustment); Article 7.1; Schedule A; Article 5.5)
  The CPI/3% annual adjustment cap in Article 7.2 applies to 'the fixed-fee components of the Services set out in Schedule A (excluding pass-through costs)'. This correctly walls off the fuel pump-price pass-through and toll authority charge pass-through, which are billed at actual third-party cost (Schedule A; Art. 7.1). However, the exclusion is incomplete on two fronts relevant to a fleet-pricing review. (1) INSURANCE: The Rate-Class insurance premium is a fixed-fee line in Schedule A, but Article 5.5 lets Wheels re-price it separately once per 12 months for 'claims experience, insurance market conditions, or vehicle risk classification' with NO cap. So the insurance premium is effectively market-driven yet is not carved out of the CPI cap logic in a clean way — it can rise under both Art. 5.5 (uncapped) and arguably Art. 7.2, and there is no anti-double-dip language. Fuel/toll are genuinely pass-through and self-adjust with actual cost, so a CPI cap on them was never the risk; insurance is the real market-driven cost that is under-controlled. (2) The fuel/toll/insurance program ADMINISTRATION fees (per-transaction admin fee, transponder account fee, mileage log fee, PDW fee) are fixed fees and are properly subject to the CPI cap — good. Net: the intent to exclude pass-throughs from CPI escalation is present and largely sound for fuel and toll, but the treatment of insurance is ambiguous and uncapped.
  → _Recommendation:_ As the Client-side pricing reviewer, tighten Article 7.2/5.5 so the escalation architecture is explicit: (a) confirm in Art. 7.2 that fuel pump-price, toll authority charges, and insurance claims/premium cost pass-throughs are entirely outside the CPI/3% cap (fuel and toll already are); (b) negotiate a cap or collar on the Article 5.5 uncapped insurance Rate-Class re-pricing — fleet-market norm is to tie insurance premium changes to the actual carrier program change plus a documented loss-ratio trigger, with a not-to-exceed annual step (e.g., 10-15%) and a right to re-market coverage or terminate the Insurance Program under Art. 12.3 if the increase exceeds that; (c) add anti-stacking language so a Vehicle's insurance premium cannot be increased under both Art. 5.5 and Art. 7.2 in the same 12-month window; (d) require Wheels to evidence the actual third-party cost change on any extraordinary/insurance adjustment so 'pass-through' remains genuinely pass-through and un-marked-up (consistent with Art. 7.1).
- ⚠️ **[HIGH] Extraordinary adjustment / service-termination right is operationally workable against the MLA** (ref: Article 7.3 (Extraordinary Adjustments); Article 12.3 (Termination of Individual Services); Article 6.6; Article 5.5; Master Lease Agreement (companion))
  Article 7.3 gives Wheels a broad extraordinary rate-adjustment right on 90 days' notice for 'material and sustained' cost changes (fuel disruption, insurance hardening), and the Client's only remedy is to terminate the affected Service under Art. 12.3 before the effective date. As a fleet-finance matter this is only partly workable because of a timing and cross-agreement mismatch: (1) NOTICE/DECISION WINDOW: Art. 7.3 gives 90 days' notice and the Client must terminate under Art. 12.3, which itself requires 30 days' notice — workable arithmetically, but the Client must decide and source alternatives inside that window. (2) INSURANCE IS EFFECTIVELY NON-TERMINABLE: If the extraordinary adjustment hits the Insurance Program (the express example – 'insurance market hardening'), Art. 12.3 forbids discontinuing insurance on any Vehicle that 'remains leased and in operation under the Master Lease Agreement' unless the Client demonstrates replacement coverage meeting the MLA minimums. Because the Vehicles stay on lease under the companion MLA regardless of what happens under this MSA (Art. 12.4 confirms MSA termination does not terminate the MLA), the Client cannot practically exit an insurance price increase — it must either accept the uncapped increase or arrange conforming replacement insurance fleet-wide within the window. That makes the 'terminate the affected Service' remedy largely illusory for the very cost (insurance) most likely to trigger Art. 7.3. (3) NO CAP / NO SYMMETRY: Art. 7.3 has no ceiling and no downward-adjustment obligation when the extraordinary condition reverses (e.g., fuel market normalizes, insurance softens), unlike a normal fleet pass-through which floats both ways. (4) SCOPE CREEP: 'material and sustained' and 'outside the ordinary course' are undefined, giving Wheels wide discretion.
  → _Recommendation:_ Make the exit remedy real and MLA-aligned: (a) For an Art. 7.3 increase to the Insurance Program, either (i) give the Client a defined cure/re-market period longer than the standard 30 days (e.g., 60-90 days) to source MLA-compliant replacement coverage without breaching the MLA insurance covenant, or (ii) obtain a matching companion carve-out in the MLA permitting the Client to self-insure/third-party insure at MLA minimums without penalty when triggered by a Wheels extraordinary adjustment — otherwise Art. 12.3's insurance proviso neutralizes the remedy. (b) Define objective triggers for 'material and sustained' (e.g., a named fuel or insurance index moving beyond a stated threshold for a stated number of consecutive months) and require Wheels to document the actual cost driver. (c) Add symmetry: require Wheels to reduce the adjusted rate when the extraordinary condition abates, and cap the extraordinary step per event. (d) Confirm the 90-day Art. 7.3 notice runs concurrently with (not before) the Art. 12.3 termination notice so the Client has the full window to decide, and that no early-termination fee under the MSA or MLA is triggered by exiting a Service in response to a Wheels-initiated extraordinary adjustment.

## Liability Data

- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Liability cap scope (what's excluded, e.g. Rentals) is intentional and doesn't create a coverage gap** (ref: Article 11.2; cross-ref Article 6.1, Article 5.5, Schedule A)
  The aggregate liability cap is set at 'TOTAL FEES PAID OR PAYABLE BY THE CLIENT UNDER THIS AGREEMENT (EXCLUDING RENTALS UNDER THE MASTER LEASE AGREEMENT) DURING THE TWELVE (12) MONTHS PRECEDING THE EVENT.' Excluding Rentals from the fee base is directionally sensible because Rentals are Master Lease consideration, not Service fees, and are governed by the companion MLA. However, two coverage-gap concerns arise that appear unintended: (1) The cap base is Client-paid Service fees, but the largest dollar-value Service the Client pays for under THIS Agreement is the Insurance Program premium (billed monthly with Rental per Schedule A). Because Insurance premiums ARE Service fees paid under this Agreement, they are inside the cap base — yet Wheels' actual exposure for an insurance-administration failure (e.g. failing to bind coverage, misclassifying Rate Class, allowing a lapse under Art. 6.6/12.3) could vastly exceed 12 months of premiums. The cap could leave the Client materially under-compensated for an uninsured-loss scenario that is squarely a fleet-management-industry risk. Art. 11.3 preserves Insurance Program _benefits payable_ but does NOT preserve a damages claim against Wheels for negligent _administration_ of the program (e.g. failure to procure). (2) 'Fees paid or payable ... during the twelve months preceding the event' produces a near-zero cap in the first year for a newly onboarded Fleet, when onboarding/billing-setup errors (the Agreement's own Article 4/Schedule C manual process) are most likely.
  → _Recommendation:_ Confirm the Rentals exclusion is intentional (it is defensible since Rentals sit under the MLA) but add an express carve-out from the cap for Wheels' failure to procure, bind, or maintain coverage under the Insurance Program and for Rate-Class/premium administration errors, mirroring the Art. 11.3 logic so the Client is not limited to ~12 months of premiums when facing an uninsured physical-damage or liability loss. Also add a floor to the cap (e.g. greater of trailing-12-month Service fees or a fixed dollar amount) to close the first-year near-zero-cap gap given the manual onboarding process. Clarify whether Insurance premiums count toward the fee base — for a fleet client the premium stream is often the single largest Service charge, so its inclusion meaningfully changes the cap size.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Confidentiality/data clauses satisfy telematics/GPS privacy obligations in the operating jurisdiction** (ref: Article 10.1, 10.2, 10.3; cross-ref Article 5.4 (which mis-references 'Article 9'))
  The data clauses are materially thin for a telematics/GPS fleet product and do not squarely satisfy the privacy obligations that attach to continuous location and driver-behavior tracking. Specific defects: (1) Telematics captures precise geolocation and individualized driver-behavior data about identifiable individuals (drivers/employees), which is regulated personal information under CCPA/CPRA (precise geolocation is a 'sensitive personal information' category), Illinois/other biometric-adjacent regimes, and various state driver-privacy and employee-monitoring notice laws. Article 10 treats everything as generic 'Confidential Information' / 'Client data' and never acknowledges driver personal data, data-subject rights, breach notification, retention limits, or lawful-basis/consent. (2) There is NO data breach notification obligation anywhere — Art. 10.3 only requires 'commercially reasonable' safeguards; every U.S. state breach-notification statute requires prompt notice, and a fleet handling driver PII must contractually allocate that duty. (3) No data retention or deletion limits on telematics/location history, and no return/destruction on termination (Art. 12.4 is silent on data). (4) Art. 10.2 permits use for 'ensuring driver safety' and 'complying with applicable law' and only prohibits _selling_ to unaffiliated third parties 'for their independent marketing' — this still allows broad secondary use and sharing (non-sale disclosures, affiliate use) that CCPA/CPRA and driver-monitoring consent laws may restrict. (5) Art. 5.4 references 'Article 9 (Confidentiality and Data Protection)' but the data-protection article is actually Article 10 — a cross-reference error that undermines the governing data clause. (6) No allocation of responsibility for obtaining driver notice/consent to GPS monitoring, which several states (e.g. requirements around employee GPS tracking notice) and general employment-privacy law require.
  → _Recommendation:_ Add a dedicated telematics/driver-data provision that: (a) defines 'Driver Personal Data' (precise geolocation, driver-behavior/scoring, diagnostics tied to an individual) separately from generic Confidential Information; (b) allocates to the Client the obligation to provide legally required notice to, and obtain any required consent from, drivers for GPS/behavior monitoring, with Wheels acting as a processor/service provider acting only on documented instructions; (c) adds a specific breach-notification clause (notice without undue delay, cooperation, and compliance with applicable state breach-notification statutes); (d) adds retention limits and a return/deletion-on-termination obligation for telematics/location history, and ties it into Art. 12.4; (e) narrows Art. 10.2 to prohibit all secondary use/sharing beyond providing the Services and legal compliance, and adds explicit CCPA/CPRA 'service provider' restrictions (no sale, no sharing, no combining) rather than only barring sale-for-marketing; (f) fixes the Art. 5.4 cross-reference from 'Article 9' to 'Article 10.' Confirm the operating jurisdiction(s) and tailor to the specific state driver-monitoring and consumer-privacy regimes where the Fleet operates.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Insurance claims carve-out from the liability cap is legally sound in the governing state** (ref: Article 11.3; cross-ref Article 11.2, 14.1 (Governing State blank))
  Article 11.3 provides that nothing in Article 11 limits or reduces 'the coverage or benefits payable under the Insurance Program' and clarifies the cap applies to inter-party claims, not to 'first- or third-party insurance claims properly payable under the Insurance Program.' The intent — that a fleet client's insurance recovery is not capped by the Service-fee liability cap — is sound and important. But the carve-out has gaps that affect legal soundness: (1) It only preserves 'benefits payable ... in accordance with its terms,' i.e. it protects the _policy proceeds_, but it does NOT preserve a _breach/negligence claim against Wheels_ for failing to arrange or maintain the coverage in the first place (see Art. 5.5 obligation to 'arrange and maintain ... coverage at all times'). If Wheels fails to bind coverage and no policy benefit exists to be 'payable,' the Client's only remedy is a capped Article 11.2 claim — the carve-out does not reach it. (2) 'Insurance Program' benefits are ultimately paid by a third-party insurer/policy, not by Wheels; the carve-out is only meaningful to the extent Wheels self-insures or administers claims, and its interaction with the Physical Damage Waiver (Art. 5.5) and the Master Lease exclusions (gross negligence, willful misconduct, unauthorized use) is not reconciled. (3) The Governing State is left blank (Art. 14.1), so soundness cannot be confirmed — most states enforce mutually negotiated commercial liability caps and carve-outs, but a few scrutinize caps that purport to limit liability for the provider's own negligence, and anti-indemnity/insurance-procurement statutes in some states affect how insurance-related obligations can be limited.
  → _Recommendation:_ Expand Art. 11.3 so the carve-out covers not only policy benefits 'payable' but also the Client's claim against Wheels for failure to procure, bind, or maintain the coverage Wheels committed to under Art. 5.5 (i.e. exclude that failure-to-insure exposure from the Art. 11.2 cap, consistent with the liability-cap recommendation above). Reconcile the carve-out expressly with the Physical Damage Waiver and the Master Lease exclusions so it is clear which losses flow through insurance vs. remain the Client's risk. Populate the Governing State in Art. 14.1 and re-confirm enforceability of both the cap and the carve-out under that state's law, checking for any anti-indemnity/insurance-procurement statute that could affect the insurance-related limitation.

## Termination Boilerplate

- ✅ **[MEDIUM] Termination of the MSA vs MLA interaction is intentional, not an oversight** (ref: Article 12.4; also 1.2 (conflict rule), 2.1, 12.3)
  The MSA deliberately and repeatedly addresses the MSA/MLA relationship on termination. Section 12.4 expressly states that 'termination of this Agreement shall not, by itself, terminate the Master Lease Agreement, which shall continue to be governed by its own terms.' This is reinforced by Section 2.1 (Wheels remains lessor/owner), the conflict-precedence rule in 1.2 (MLA prevails on leasing/title/return; MSA prevails on billing of Services), and the Insurance carve-outs in 6.6, 12.3, and 8.1 that keep coverage in place while a Vehicle remains leased and operating. This is clearly an intentional decoupling, not an oversight, and it is the correct posture for fleet management: the lease (an asset-financing instrument with residual/title stakes) should survive the termination of the ancillary services wrapper. The one substantive gap worth flagging is the reverse direction: the MSA does not say what happens to the Services if the MLA terminates or a Vehicle is returned/remarketed early. Since Services are defined only by reference to Vehicles 'leased by the Client from Wheels under the Master Lease Agreement' (defn of Fleet/Vehicle), a Vehicle exiting the lease should automatically drop out of billing, but this is left to implication.
  → _Recommendation:_ Confirm the decoupling is deliberate (it reads as such) and add a short reciprocal sentence to 12.4: upon termination of the MLA or return/remarketing of a Vehicle, that Vehicle is automatically de-enrolled from all Services and Service billing ceases as of the return/disposition date, with a final reconciliation of usage-based charges (fuel, toll, excess mileage per 5.2 Lease-end true-up) and any transponder/telematics device recovery. Also state the Insurance Program continuation obligation ends only when Wheels ceases to be the insured owner, to avoid a gap where Wheels bills insurance on a Vehicle that has left the fleet. This closes the one-directional silence without disturbing the correct MSA-survives-MLA logic.
- ⚠️ **[MEDIUM] Assignment clause balances Wheels' M&A flexibility against client counterparty risk** (ref: Article 16.3)
  16.3 gives Wheels a broad free-assignment right without consent to an affiliate or 'in connection with a merger, acquisition, or sale of substantially all of its assets relating to this Agreement,' subject only to the assignee assuming Wheels' obligations. This is appropriate and market-standard for a fleet lessor/servicer that expects portfolio-level M&A and securitization activity, so Wheels' M&A flexibility is well protected. The imbalance is on the client-protection side. In fleet management the client is not merely buying a fungible service: it is entrusting its vehicle telematics/location data, fuel and mileage records, driver-behavior and safety data (Article 10), and relying on the servicer's continued insurance-program arrangements (5.5). A bare 'assignee assumes obligations' covenant does not protect the Client if the Services are assigned to a competitor of the Client, to a servicer of materially lesser operational capability, or to an affiliate that is a shell without the balance sheet to stand behind the Insurance Program and indemnities. There is also no requirement that the assignee be capable of maintaining the licensed telematics platform, fuel-network access, or insurance arrangements the Client depends on, and no notice-to-Client obligation on assignment.
  → _Recommendation:_ Rebalance 16.3 while preserving Wheels' M&A latitude: (1) keep the affiliate/M&A free-assignment right but require the assignee to have operational and financial capacity to perform (specifically to maintain the Insurance Program, telematics platform, and fuel/toll network access) and to be bound by Article 10 data-protection covenants; (2) add a carve-out permitting the Client to terminate the affected Services on short notice (e.g., 30-60 days) without penalty if the MSA is assigned to a direct competitor of the Client or to an entity that cannot demonstrate equivalent service capability; and (3) require prompt written notice to the Client of any assignment. For an affiliate assignment, consider requiring Wheels (or ultimate parent) to remain guarantor of the insurance and indemnity obligations so the Client is not left facing a thinly-capitalized affiliate. These are fleet-specific protections (data, insurance continuity, competitor concern), not generic anti-assignment boilerplate.
- ❌ **[HIGH] Arbitration seat/institution placeholders are flagged as required-before-execution** (ref: Article 14.3; also 14.1 [Governing State])
  The arbitration clause contains two unfilled critical placeholders that make the dispute-resolution mechanism unworkable as drafted. 14.3 refers to 'the commercial arbitration rules of a mutually agreed arbitral institution' (institution never named) and a tribunal 'seated in [City, State]' (seat left blank). 'Mutually agreed institution' with no default means that, absent agreement at the time of a dispute, there is no functioning arbitration mechanism to compel - a party in breach can simply refuse to agree, defeating the clause. The blank seat leaves the lex arbitri and supervisory courts undetermined, which affects enforceability, interim relief, and appeal rights. This is compounded by 14.1, where [Governing State] is also blank. For a multi-jurisdiction fleet operator that will litigate billing/insurance disputes repeatedly, an indeterminate seat and institution is a material execution-readiness defect, not a drafting nicety.
  → _Recommendation:_ Treat this as a hard gate before execution: replace the placeholders with a named administering institution and rules (e.g., AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules for a US fleet operation) and a fixed seat (typically aligned with the [Governing State] in 14.1 and Wheels' principal place of business for portfolio consistency), and fill in the governing state in 14.1. Add a fallback default so the clause is self-executing even if the parties do not 'mutually agree' (e.g., 'failing agreement within 15 days, the arbitration shall be administered by [named default institution] under its Commercial Rules, seated in [default city, state]'). Wheels should standardize seat/institution across its client contracts to avoid a fragmented dispute-resolution footprint across the fleet portfolio. Do not send to signature until both 14.1 and 14.3 blanks are completed.
